Market Basics

Stock (Share / Equity)
A unit of ownership in a company. Buying stock makes you a part-owner (shareholder) entitled to a portion of profits and assets.
Example: Owning 10 AAPL shares means you own a tiny slice of Apple Inc.
Bull Market
A period of rising stock prices, typically defined as a 20%+ gain from a recent low. Associated with investor optimism and economic growth.
Example: The S&P 500 ran in a bull market for most of 2009–2020.
Bear Market
A period of falling stock prices — a 20%+ decline from a recent high. Reflects pessimism, often tied to economic downturns.
Example: The COVID-19 crash in March 2020 briefly entered bear-market territory.
Market Correction
A decline of 10–20% in a stock or index from its recent peak. Less severe than a bear market; often considered healthy.
Market Capitalization (Market Cap)
The total market value of a company's outstanding shares. Calculated as: Share Price × Total Shares Outstanding.
Example: If a stock trades at $50 and has 1B shares, market cap = $50B.
Stock Exchange
A marketplace where stocks are bought and sold. Major US exchanges include the NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) and NASDAQ.
Index
A benchmark tracking a group of stocks to represent overall market performance. Common examples: S&P 500, Dow Jones Industrial Average, NASDAQ Composite.
IPO (Initial Public Offering)
When a private company first sells shares to the public on a stock exchange, raising capital and allowing public ownership.
Example: Google went public via IPO in August 2004.
Ticker Symbol
A short abbreviation used to identify a publicly traded company on an exchange.
Example: AAPL = Apple, TSLA = Tesla, MSFT = Microsoft.
Trading Volume
The number of shares traded during a given period. High volume often signals strong investor interest or news-driven activity.

Price & Returns

Bid / Ask Price
The bid is the highest price a buyer will pay; the ask is the lowest price a seller will accept. The difference is the spread.
52-Week High / Low
The highest and lowest price a stock has traded at over the past 52 weeks. Used to gauge current price relative to its recent range.
All-Time High (ATH)
The highest price a stock has ever reached in its entire trading history.
Day Change (%)
The percentage the stock price moved from yesterday's closing price to today's current (or closing) price.
Capital Gains
Profit earned when you sell a stock for more than you paid for it. Short-term (held <1 year) is taxed as ordinary income; long-term at a lower rate.
Return on Investment (ROI)
The gain or loss on an investment relative to its cost, expressed as a percentage: (Gain − Cost) / Cost × 100.
Example: Buy at $100, sell at $130 → ROI = 30%.
Volatility
How much a stock's price fluctuates over time. High volatility = large price swings. Often measured by standard deviation or the VIX index.
Stock Split
When a company divides its existing shares into multiple new shares, lowering the price per share while keeping total value the same.
Example: A 4-for-1 split turns 1 share at $400 into 4 shares at $100 each.

Valuation Metrics

P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
Stock price divided by earnings per share (EPS). Indicates how much investors pay per dollar of earnings. A high P/E may mean the stock is expensive or high-growth.
Example: Stock at $50, EPS = $2 → P/E = 25.
EPS (Earnings Per Share)
Net profit divided by the number of outstanding shares. Higher EPS generally signals better profitability.
P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)
Stock price divided by book value per share (total assets minus liabilities). Useful for valuing asset-heavy companies like banks.
P/S Ratio (Price-to-Sales)
Market cap divided by total revenue. Useful for companies that aren't yet profitable — especially early-stage growth stocks.
Forward P/E
Like the P/E ratio but uses projected (future) earnings instead of historical ones. Reflects market expectations for growth.
PEG Ratio
P/E divided by the expected earnings growth rate. A PEG of 1 is considered fair value; below 1 may indicate undervaluation.
Intrinsic Value
An estimate of what a stock is actually worth based on fundamentals (earnings, growth, risk), independent of its current market price.
Book Value
A company's total assets minus total liabilities — what shareholders would theoretically receive if the company were liquidated today.
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A proxy for operating profitability, stripping out financing and accounting decisions.

Dividends & Income

Dividend
A portion of company profits paid out to shareholders, usually quarterly. Not all stocks pay dividends — growth companies often reinvest earnings instead.
Dividend Yield
Annual dividend per share divided by the stock price, expressed as a percentage. Shows how much income you earn relative to price.
Example: $2 annual dividend on a $40 stock → 5% yield.
Ex-Dividend Date
You must own the stock before this date to receive the upcoming dividend. The stock price typically drops by roughly the dividend amount on this date.
Payout Ratio
Percentage of earnings paid as dividends. A very high ratio (>80%) may be unsustainable; low means more reinvestment into growth.
DRIP (Dividend Reinvestment Plan)
Automatically reinvesting dividends to buy more shares instead of receiving cash. Compounds returns over time.

Order Types & Trading

Market Order
Buy or sell a stock immediately at the best available current price. Fast execution but price is not guaranteed.
Limit Order
Buy or sell only at a specific price or better. Gives price control but may not execute if the market doesn't reach your limit.
Example: Limit buy at $95 — only fills if the price drops to $95 or below.
Stop-Loss Order
Automatically sells a stock when it falls to a specified price, limiting your downside loss.
Example: Buy at $100, set stop-loss at $85 — auto-sells if price drops to $85.
Stop-Limit Order
Combines a stop trigger with a limit price. When the stop triggers, a limit order is placed — giving more price control than a stop-loss alone.
Short Selling
Borrowing shares and selling them, hoping to buy them back cheaper later. Profits from falling prices but has unlimited loss potential.
Margin Trading
Using borrowed money from your broker to buy more stock than you could with your own capital alone. Amplifies both gains and losses.
Day Trading
Buying and selling stocks within the same trading day, never holding overnight. Requires active monitoring and carries high risk.
Liquidity
How easily a stock can be bought or sold without significantly affecting its price. Large-cap stocks are highly liquid; penny stocks often are not.

Analysis & Strategy

Fundamental Analysis
Evaluating a stock based on financial data — earnings, revenue, growth rates, balance sheet — to determine its intrinsic value.
Technical Analysis
Using historical price charts and patterns (moving averages, RSI, MACD) to predict future price movements.
Moving Average (MA)
The average closing price over a set number of days (e.g. 50-day, 200-day MA). Smooths out noise to show trend direction.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
A momentum indicator (0–100) showing if a stock is overbought (>70) or oversold (<30). Helps identify potential reversal points.
Support & Resistance
Support: a price level where buying interest is strong enough to stop a decline. Resistance: a level where selling pressure tends to stop a rise.
Diversification
Spreading investments across different assets, sectors, or geographies to reduce risk. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)
Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of price. Reduces impact of volatility by buying more shares when prices are low.
Hedge
An investment taken to reduce the risk of adverse price movements in another investment. Options and inverse ETFs are common hedging tools.
Portfolio Rebalancing
Periodically adjusting holdings back to target allocations after price changes have shifted the proportions.

Investment Instruments

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A basket of securities (stocks, bonds, commodities) that trades on an exchange like a single stock. Offers diversification at low cost.
Example: SPY tracks the S&P 500 index.
Mutual Fund
A pooled investment vehicle managed by professionals. Unlike ETFs, mutual funds are priced once per day after market close.
Bond
A debt instrument where you lend money to a company or government in exchange for periodic interest payments and return of principal at maturity.
Option
A contract giving the right (not obligation) to buy (call) or sell (put) a stock at a specified price before expiration.
Futures
A contract to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a future date. Common in commodities, indexes, and currencies.
REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)
Companies that own income-producing real estate. Trade like stocks and must pay at least 90% of taxable income as dividends.
Index Fund
A passive fund designed to replicate the performance of a market index (like the S&P 500). Known for low fees and broad diversification.
Blue Chip Stock
Shares of large, well-established, financially stable companies with a long track record. Generally considered lower risk.
Example: Apple, Johnson & Johnson, JPMorgan Chase.
Penny Stock
Shares trading below $5 (sometimes defined as below $1). Often small companies with low liquidity and high volatility — high risk, high reward potential.

Risk & Psychology

Risk Tolerance
The degree of variability in investment returns you're willing to accept. Influenced by age, income, goals, and emotional temperament.
FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
Emotional bias that causes investors to buy into rising assets impulsively for fear of missing gains — often near market tops.
FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt)
Negative sentiment spread about an asset, causing panic selling. Can be legitimate concerns or deliberate market manipulation.
Drawdown
The peak-to-trough decline during a specific period. A 30% drawdown means a portfolio fell 30% from its highest point.
Beta
Measures a stock's volatility relative to the market. Beta >1 = more volatile than market; <1 = less volatile.
Example: Beta of 1.5 means the stock tends to move 50% more than the index.
Alpha
The excess return an investment generates above its benchmark. Positive alpha means outperformance; it's the goal of active management.
Sharpe Ratio
Return minus risk-free rate, divided by standard deviation. Measures return per unit of risk. Higher is better.
Margin Call
When a broker demands you deposit more funds because your margin account has fallen below the required minimum — triggered by large losses on leveraged positions.
